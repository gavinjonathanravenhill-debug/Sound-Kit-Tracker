Digging Deep

How I spent three years building a full basement under an existing house — by hand, by machine, and by sheer bloody-mindedness.

Scroll
01 — The Idea

What if there was a whole floor under here?

Most people extend outward or upward. I decided to go down.

The house sits on a standard brick foundation near Guildford in Surrey — nothing special, nothing designed for conversion. But there was space beneath the ground floor: old footings, clay soil, and the ghost of a crawl space that had been filled in decades ago. The plan was ambitious but simple in concept: excavate the earth from beneath the existing house and create a fully usable lower ground floor.

Simple to say. Three years to do. What follows is the story of that dig — the surveys, the trenches, the underpinning, the flooding, the steel, and the moment you finally stand in a room that didn't exist when you started.

02 — Surveying the Footings

Measuring what's already there

Before a single shovel hit dirt, every footing had to be mapped.

The existing foundations needed to be fully understood — their depths, widths, and condition at every point around the perimeter. I surveyed each footing location and recorded three key measurements: A (level/height in mm), B (footing width), and C (footing depth). These varied significantly around the building, from 130mm to 240mm wide, and 292mm to 600mm deep.

Left: Hand-drawn footing survey with measurements at each perimeter point (A–K). Right: The proposed lower ground floor layout from Rivergate Consultants — marked clearly as "NOT for construction" pending structural calculations.

The architectural drawings showed the proposed layout — a stair void, reinforced concrete slab areas, new 215mm block walls at lower ground level, and a window opening to be widened with a new RSJ. The overall clear footprint came in around 6,081mm × 6,545mm — a serious amount of usable space.

"No structural calculations have been undertaken yet. This drawing is NOT for construction."

— Note on the Rivergate Consultants layout, September 2018

03 — The Dig

Breaking ground — everywhere

Once the design was locked, it was time to start digging. And digging. And digging.

The front elevation fully excavated, exposing the original brick foundation wall down to footing level. Cables, pipes, and root systems all had to be carefully worked around.

The excavation started from the outside — opening up trenches along the front and side of the house to expose the existing foundations. What you find when you dig next to a house that's stood for decades is a history lesson in brick, mortar, backfill, and buried services. Every metre uncovered something — old drain runs, root systems, cables that shouldn't have been where they were.

Left: A narrow trench cut along the front wall for meter relocation — concrete path broken out to access services below. Right: The exterior during mid-project — backfilled and patched, but far from finished.

The gas and electric meters needed relocating, which meant trenching through the front pathway and dealing with the utility companies on their timeline, not yours.

The full front elevation during initial dig-out — tools, rubble, and the early stages of what would become a three-year project.

04 — Getting the Dirt Out

The conveyor solution

You can't dig a basement without somewhere for the earth to go — and you can't carry it up stairs that don't exist yet.

The biggest logistical challenge of any basement dig is spoil removal. You're underground, surrounded by walls, with tonnes of heavy clay that needs to get from down there to out there. The answer? Miniveyor conveyor belts — industrial-grade belt conveyors designed for exactly this kind of restricted-access earthwork.

Two Miniveyor units stacked on the driveway, ready for deployment. These motorised belt conveyors are purpose-built for moving spoil out of confined basement spaces.

The conveyors were fed through openings knocked into the exterior brick wall. From inside, one conveyor angled upward through the wall opening and connected to a second unit outside, which carried the spoil up and out to the muck-away pile.

Inside the cellar space during the dig — conveyor belt running along the floor toward the wall opening, plywood boards guiding the spoil. Work light, shovel, and heavy clay everywhere.
Left: Outside — conveyors running out of the wall openings, external trench visible below. Right: Where it all ends up. The spoil heap on the driveway — tonnes of Surrey clay, rubble, and broken brick, waiting for muck-away.

It's slow, noisy, messy work. The Surrey clay is heavy and sticky — it clogs everything, weighs a tonne, and doesn't shift easily. But metre by metre, bucket by bucket, the basement floor dropped and the headroom grew.

05 — Underpinning

Holding the house up

You can't just dig the ground out from under a house and hope for the best. Every existing footing had to be underpinned.

Underpinning is the structural heart of any basement conversion. The existing footings were never designed to carry the building above a void — they were designed to sit on solid ground. With that ground now being removed, new concrete had to be poured beneath the existing foundations in carefully sequenced sections, extending them down to the new formation level.

The heavy end of the job — a Pyramid Steel RSJ spanning the opening, with a mini digger working the excavation beneath. Temporary props, Acrows, and timber needle beams supporting the structure while the underpinning goes in.

This was not a tidy process. A mini excavator worked inside the basement, digging out sections beneath the existing footings while the building above was temporarily supported on steel beams, Acrow props, and timber needles. Each section was dug, shuttered, and poured before moving to the next — you never underpin adjacent sections at the same time.

Left: An underpinning pit dug to depth — waterlogged, as they often were. Right: Close-up beneath an existing footing — you can see the original concrete strip with the new excavation underneath, ready to be filled with concrete. Mini digger visible at the right edge.
The supply chain — pallets of Rugby fast-set cement, trade bags of sand and aggregate stacked on the drive. Behind them, the spoil heap and dismantled conveyor sections. A building site in a residential cul-de-sac.

06 — Fighting the Water

When Surrey floods

Dig a big hole in clay ground and it will fill with water. That's not a risk — it's a certainty.

The water table in the Guildford area sits higher than you'd like when you're trying to build underground. Every time it rained — and this is England, so that's often — the excavation filled up. Groundwater seeped through the clay, surface water ran in through the wall openings, and the whole site turned into a swimming pool.

The low point — literally. The basement completely flooded, mini digger partially submerged, conveyor belt underwater. Everything stops until this gets pumped out.

Pumps ran constantly. Submersible pumps sat in sumps at the lowest points, running around the clock to keep the water level manageable. But sometimes the rain won. The photo above shows one of the worst days — the mini digger sitting in muddy water, tools submerged, and the whole project on pause until the pumps could clear it.

This is the part they don't show you on Grand Designs. The unglamorous, frustrating, weather-dependent reality of working below ground level. You pump it out, you clean up, and you crack on.

07 — The Slab

From a hole to a floor

Once the underpinning was complete and the formation level reached, it was time to build the reinforced concrete slab.

The full floor area laid with steel reinforcement mesh, raised on spacers, with a sump pump still working in the centre. Two wall openings visible — the former conveyor routes. Laser level set up on the back wall. Orange spray marks on the brick indicating reference levels.

The rebar mesh covered the entire floor area — a double layer of steel reinforcement sitting on spacers above the blinding, ready for the concrete pour. The sump pump stayed in place right up until the pour, keeping the water at bay. Getting concrete down into a basement through wall openings isn't straightforward either — it came in via a pump line, filling the shuttered floor section by section.

Earlier in the process — the earth partially excavated inside, services exposed, and daylight coming through the newly formed wall opening.

With the slab poured and cured, the space transformed. No longer a muddy pit — now a solid, level, reinforced concrete floor. The new block walls went up, the stair void was formed, and upstairs the ceiling structure took shape.

Plasterboard going up on the ceiling above — taped joints, screw patterns visible, oak framing around the new stair opening. The transition from building site to liveable space.

Built from below

Not every project goes sideways or upwards. Sometimes the best space in the house is the one you dig yourself.

Three years of weekends, evenings, planning arguments, clay under fingernails, flooded excavations, and conversations with building control. But at the end of it: a whole new floor. A room that didn't exist. Built not by extending the footprint of the house, but by going beneath it — one shovel, one conveyor belt, one stubborn decision at a time.
